How Pittsburgh Regional Transit tracks its buses and where the system breaks down
Pittsburgh Regional Transit relies on a 25-year partnership with Clever Devices for all GPS tracking, real-time predictions, and onboard intelligence across its 700-bus, 83-light-rail fleet — but manual driver logins, cellular dead zones, and an inability to display detours create persistent "ghost bus" problems that keep on-time performance stuck at 66%. The system processes nearly 4 million tracking requests every weekday Mass Transit and feeds data to the Transit app (used by ~40% of daily riders), TransitPittsburghforpublictransit yet structural gaps in the data pipeline mean roughly one in five bus arrivals can fail to appear on any tracker. PRT's technology stack is among the most mature of its mid-size Rust Belt peers, but a looming $100 million funding crisis Pittsburgh Regional Transit threatens to stall upgrades just as the agency rolls out new disruption management tools.

The GPS requires drivers to log in, and that single step creates ghost buses
The most consequential design choice in PRT's tracking system is that GPS tracking requires manual operator login at the start of each run. The IVN's GPS receiver may power on with the vehicle, but data transmission to the TrueTime system only begins when the driver authenticates via the Transit Control Head — a dashboard-mounted interface where they select their route, run, and block assignment. As PRT Director of Transportation Technology Scott Vetere told PublicSource in April 2024: "Operators must log on to the GPS at the beginning of their ride so the system can send out location data and arrival information to riders." PublicSourcePublicSource
When an operator skips this step — whether from oversight, time pressure, or equipment issues — the bus becomes invisible. It runs its route, picks up and drops off passengers, but generates no position data. Riders see a scheduled arrival on their app that never materializes: a ghost bus. PublicSource A 2015 Pittsburgh Post-Gazette field test of 200 bus arrivals found that while TrueTime was accurate 96% of the time when GPS was functioning, fully 20% of observed arrivals never appeared on the system at all — GPS was off, broken, or absent. That dropped effective accuracy to roughly 72%. govtechGovernment Technology
Once logged in, the IVN transmits position data via cellular networks every 30 seconds. Third-party apps typically display updates at 30–40 second intervals, PublicSourcePublicSource though some may interpolate between pings. Bulk data (APC counts, video, software updates) downloads over Wi-Fi when vehicles return to the depot. Vetere identified three root causes of tracking failures: operators not logging in, patchy cellular coverage, and buses deviating from programmed paths. PublicSource
No publicly available ATU Local 85 contract language or grievance records address the GPS login requirement specifically, though the union's willingness to challenge unilateral management directives, such as its 2022 Pennsylvania Labor Relations Board complaint over a vaccine mandate, suggests technology-related work rules could become a bargaining issue.

TrueTime runs on BusTime's hybrid prediction engine, but detours break it
PRT's public-facing TrueTime system, accessible at truetime.portauthority.org — is a direct deployment of Clever Devices' BusTime product for buses Cleverdevices and TAPS for light rail. Cleverdevices The prediction engine uses a hybrid model combining GPS position, schedule data, and pattern-distance tracking. The BusTime API exposes the mechanics: each vehicle reports its linear distance traveled into its route pattern Cleverdevices (pdist), and the server calculates remaining distance to each downstream stop (dstp). Predicted arrival times blend scheduled travel times between stops with the vehicle's real-time speed and progress. A boolean delay flag (dly) triggers when a vehicle has been stationary beyond a threshold, adjusting predictions upward. smtd
For light rail, TAPS adds complexity by incorporating track occupancy data, travel restrictions, and service changes alongside GPS positioning Cleverdevices — critical for Pittsburgh's system, which includes underground segments downtown and through Mt. Washington where GPS alone is insufficient. Mass Transit
The system's most significant technical limitation is its handling of detours. PRT's own TrueTime website states plainly: "TrueTime cannot currently display detours. Detoured vehicles will not be displayed on the tracker until they return to regular routing." Portauthority When a bus leaves its programmed pattern — for construction, events, or emergencies — it enters an "off-route" state and vanishes from all rider-facing platforms. The BusTime API v3 does include detour-handling endpoints (getdetours, detour patterns, affected stop metadata), and Clever Devices' Disruption Management module allows dispatchers to create dynamic reroutes. smtd +2 But as of the most recent documentation, these capabilities had not been fully surfaced to riders. In May 2025, Clever Devices announced a partnership with the Transit app to deliver detour data via the new GTFS-RT Trip Modifications standard — a capability now available in the platform, though PRT was not named among the first agencies deploying it. On-time performance has plateaued at 66%, and a funding crisis looms
PRT's tracking and reliability problems are well-documented across multiple sources. The agency's FY 2024 Annual Service Report shows on-time performance at 66% — below its own 75% target and down from 67% the prior year. Missed trips reached 1.7% (target: 1.5% or lower), with the rate having peaked at a staggering 18% in March 2022 following vaccine mandate-related workforce departures. Total ridership slipped to 33.3 million, Pittsburgh Union Progress roughly 1% below FY 2023 and lagging the national average for post-pandemic recovery. Pittsburghforpublictransit

Conclusion
PRT's technology infrastructure tells a story of early adoption meeting structural constraints. The agency was ahead of many peers in deploying Clever Devices' full suite starting in 2000, Rail Journal +2 and its BusTime-powered TrueTime system handles millions of daily requests across bus and rail. Mass Transit But the system's Achilles' heel — manual driver login — transforms a technology problem into a human process problem that no software upgrade alone can fix. The inability to display detoured vehicles compounds this: Portauthority riders experience a tracking system that works well when conditions are normal but fails precisely when they need it most.
